# ADR-002 — Use PoR Merkle Challenge for Proof of Storage

**Status:** Accepted
**Topic:** #2 Proof of Storage
**Supersedes:** —
**Superseded by:** —
**Research source:** Papers 05, 07, 18, 29, 37, 41

---

## Context

The system must periodically verify that providers are actually storing the chunks they have been assigned. [Paper 07](../research/paper-07-sok-dsn.md) establishes a two-class Proof of Storage (PoS) taxonomy used by every production DSN: Transitory PoS (proves data was received at upload time) and Continuous PoS (proves data is continuously retained). Both classes are needed. Three mechanisms were evaluated for the continuous phase: Proof of Replication (PoRep) + Proof of Spacetime (PoSt) (used by Filecoin), Merkle challenges (used by Sia/Proof of Retrievability (PoR)), and Berlekamp-Welch random stripe audits (used by Storj (Storj's PoR)).

## Options Considered

| Option | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| PoRep + PoSt | Cryptographically robust; anti-Sybil | 256 GB RAM + GPU ≥11 GB VRAM required — eliminates all desktop users |
| Berlekamp-Welch random stripe audits | Avoids audit false positives | Stripe-level, not chunk-level; disputed by Szabó et al. |
| **PoR Merkle challenge** | Lightweight; works on consumer hardware; computationally inexpensive | Can be bypassed if challenge timing is predictable (mitigated by ADR-014) |

## Decision

Two-phase proof of storage:

1. **Transitory PoS** — at upload time, the provider signs a receipt confirming the chunks are stored. This is immediate and synchronous. Used by StorJ 
2. **Continuous PoR** — random Merkle challenges issued on an ongoing schedule. Challenge timing is randomised to prevent just-in-time retrieval attacks. Response deadline enforced: `(chunk_size / declared_upload_speed) × 1.5`.

Both phases produce signed receipts recorded in the audit trail (see [ADR-015](../decisions/ADR-015-audit-trail.md)).
 
The challenge nonce is generated by the microservice at challenge time (not pre-generated at upload time). Storj v2 used pre-generated Merkle challenges and storage nodes defeated the scheme by caching challenge-response pairs. The unlimited scheme — selecting the challenge at audit time using HMAC(server_secret, chunk_id + server_ts) — prevents any forward caching. ([Paper 05](../research/paper-05-storj.md), Section 4.13)

## Consequences

**Positive:**
- Works on all target hardware (desktop, NAS)
- Computationally inexpensive — audit overhead stays within the 5% CPU budget
- The microservice as sole challenge issuer and verifier is not merely an architectural convenience. [Paper 37](../research/paper-37-shelby-incentive-compatibility.md)  formally proves that peer-to-peer audit reports without a trusted backstop collapse to universal dishonesty as the unique Nash equilibrium. Centralising the auditor is the game-theoretic escape from this collapse.

**Negative / trade-offs:**
- Weaker than PoRep — does not prove data was physically encoded on the device
- Requires randomised challenge timing to prevent bypass (operational complexity)

**Open constraints:**
- Transparent Merkle Log (ADR-015) must be operational for proofs to be independently verifiable
- The PoR guarantee is weaker than PoRep. The three attack classes it does not close (Sybil, outsourcing, generation) are covered by ADR-014 and ADR-005 respectively. These ADRs are co-requisites for ADR-002's security argument to hold.
- Service-denial attack: a provider can store data, pass all PoR challenges, and simultaneously refuse client retrieval requests. ADR-002 does not close this attack at the audit level. The structural mitigation is RS(16,56): effective denial requires > 40 of 56 providers to refuse simultaneously. The 20% ASN cap (ADR-014) limits any correlated group to ~11 providers, well below the 40-provider threshold. See Q41-1 for whether retrieval failures should feed the reliability scorer.
