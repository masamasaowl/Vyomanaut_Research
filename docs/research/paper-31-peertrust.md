# Paper 31 — PeerTrust: Supporting Reputation-Based Trust for Peer-to-Peer Electronic Communities

**Authors:** Li Xiong, Ling Liu — Georgia Institute of Technology **Venue / Year:** IEEE Transactions on Knowledge and Data Engineering, Vol. 16, No. 7 | July 2004 **Topics:** #8, #18, #19 **ADRs produced:** none — ADR-008 confirmed with new constraints; ADR-024 gains concrete penalty-structure parameters; both decisions remain as-is

---

## Problem Solved

Reputation systems in P2P file-sharing environments fail because they aggregate ratings without accounting for how trustworthy the raters themselves are, how many transactions back the rating, what context the transaction occurred in, or whether raters are colluding. A simple sum or average of feedback (the eBay model) is gamed by malicious peers who flood the system with fake positive ratings for each other while badmouthing honest peers. PeerTrust solves this by introducing a five-parameter trust metric — feedback satisfaction, transaction count, credibility of feedback source, transaction context, and community context — and proves via simulation that the personalised-similarity credibility measure (PSM) remains effective even at 70% adversarial peer share, including against collusion, while a simpler recursive-trust-value measure (TVM) collapses above 50% adversarial share. For Vyomanaut, the paper directly feeds ADR-024 (economic mechanism): it provides the structural basis for why Vyomanaut's single-authoritative-scorer model is the only viable implementation path for V2, and it identifies the specific parameter design choices that determine whether a reputation system deters strategic reputation manipulation by providers.

## Key Findings

**The five trust parameters and why each is necessary (Section 3.1):** Feedback alone allows volume inflation: a peer completing many transactions can cheat frequently and still show a rising score. Adding transaction count normalises feedback to a per-transaction rate. Credibility of feedback source prevents fake ratings from low-trust peers from carrying equal weight. Transaction context factor weights larger or more critical transactions more heavily, defending against the strategy of building trust cheaply on small transactions then cheating on a large one. Community context factor addresses community-specific vulnerabilities such as feedback incentives and pre-trusted anchors.

**Two credibility measures with different collusion resistance (Section 3.3):** The TVM metric uses a peer's own trust score recursively as its feedback credibility weight. This fails when malicious peers constitute a majority: they form a closed trust loop, all rate each other highly, and the honest minority is outvoted. TVM error reaches 100% at 50% adversarial share in the collusive setting. The PSM metric uses personalised similarity — peer w weights peer v's feedback by how similarly v and w have rated shared acquaintances in the past. A collusive group gives consistently different ratings from honest peers, so their similarity to honest peers is low, and their feedback is filtered out. PSM maintains effectiveness up to 70% adversarial share in simulations and is fully robust against the collusive setting.

**Adaptive time-window algorithm detects dynamic personality (Section 4.3):** Malicious peers can build reputation and then exploit it (the "milking" strategy) or oscillate between building and milking. A fixed time window that discounts old feedback eventually collapses such a peer's reputation but too slowly. PeerTrust adaptive computes a second score over a smaller recent window (Is=20 vs I=100) and uses the lower value if it falls below the full-window value by a threshold. This catches reputation drop-off quickly and makes rebuilding a reputation harder than milking it once detected.

**Scalability (Section 5.5):** TVM/DTC requires O(N) network hops per computation — does not scale. TVM/ATC and PSM/ATC with caching both scale at O(log N) hops because only the target peer's feedback is fetched fresh; credibility values are cached. PSM/DTC requires O(I) additional direct lookups to retrieve raters' own feedback histories and is practical but more expensive. For a production V2 implementation, the ATC-style caching strategy is the only viable approach regardless of which metric is chosen.

**New-peer bootstrapping uses a default trust value (Section 4.2, Algorithm 1):** In TVM/DTC, all peers start at a default trust value T_default. In ATC implementations, a cache miss defaults to T_default. This is the equivalent of Vyomanaut's optimistic random assignment (ADR-005) — a new provider is treated as neutral until audit evidence accumulates.

## Trade-offs

| Chosen | Over | Consequence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| PSM (personalised similarity credibility) | TVM (recursive trust credibility) | Robust against collusion at up to 70% adversarial share; requires more lookups and rater history; cannot be computed without prior shared interaction history |
| Adaptive time window (dual-window) | Fixed time window | Detects reputation milking faster; makes rebuilding expensive; doubles computation per evaluation |
| Distributed DHT-based score storage (P-Grid) | Central server | Eliminates single point of failure; introduces trust issues at storage peers (mitigated by replication + majority voting + PKI signing) |
| Transaction-normalised feedback (ratio) | Raw feedback sum | Prevents volume-inflation strategy; a peer who cheats 25% of the time is correctly scored regardless of transaction volume |
| PKI-signed feedback with encryption to polling peer | Plain feedback transmission | Detects tampered feedback; prevents man-in-the-middle; adds per-lookup crypto overhead |

## Breaks in Our Case

- **PeerTrust requires pairwise transaction feedback between peers — peer i rates peer j after interacting with j**≠ **Vyomanaut's audit signal flows exclusively through the microservice; providers never directly rate each other** → The five-parameter trust metric cannot be applied as designed because the input — pairwise satisfaction ratings — does not exist in Vyomanaut's architecture. Audit pass/fail is generated by the microservice, not by peer-to-peer service consumption. The PSM metric requires a shared interaction history between the evaluating peer and the rater; in Vyomanaut, no such history exists between any two providers. The paper's mechanism is architecturally incompatible with V2's centralised-audit model. ADR-008's three-window rolling score remains the correct design.
- **PeerTrust's transaction context factor weights feedback by transaction size** ≠ **all Vyomanaut audit challenges are structurally identical (same chunk size lf=256 KB, same challenge format)** → Transaction context differentiation is irrelevant for storage audits where every challenge is the same type. The paper's defense against the "build trust on small transactions, cheat on large ones" attack does not apply to a homogeneous audit workload.
- **PeerTrust's collusion defense (PSM) requires that honest and malicious peers have differing interaction histories** ≠ **our 20% ASN cap (ADR-014) is the structural collusion defense, enforced at placement time not at scoring time** → PeerTrust PSM detects collusion after the fact through feedback divergence. Vyomanaut's defense is pre-emptive: colluding providers are physically prevented from holding more than 20% of any file's shards, so even if their audit scores were inflated by mutual manipulation, the data loss risk from their simultaneous departure is bounded. The two approaches are complementary in theory, but only the placement-time cap is feasible in V2 without pairwise interaction data.
- **PeerTrust's community context factor can be used to reward peers for providing feedback** ≠ **Vyomanaut pays per audit passed (ADR-012), not per feedback provided** → The incentive problem PeerTrust's community context factor addresses — why would peers bother rating each other — is eliminated by Vyomanaut's payment model. Providers are financially incentivised to pass audits; the microservice generates audit outcomes automatically without requiring peer participation in rating.
- **PeerTrust's distributed DHT storage of trust data introduces a trust problem with storage peers** ≠ **Vyomanaut's reliability scores are stored exclusively in the microservice's append-only Postgres database (ADR-015, ADR-016)** → PeerTrust must solve a meta-problem: who can be trusted to store the trust data? Majority voting and PKI signing are required. Vyomanaut's centralised score storage avoids this entirely. The cost is the microservice as a bottleneck, which is acceptable for the 6 non-I-confluent operations including score decrement (ADR-013).
- **PeerTrust simulates 128 peers with 100 transactions each** ≠ **Vyomanaut V2 targets thousands of providers with thousands of audit receipts per provider** → The simulation validates the metric's properties at small scale. The ATC caching strategy scales to O(log N) per computation, which is workable, but the requirement for shared interaction history between any evaluating node and the raters of its target is increasingly hard to satisfy as the network grows and interaction graphs become sparse. This is not a V2 concern since the microservice holds all audit history, but it would be the key scaling challenge if PeerTrust were ever adapted for V3.

## Decisions Influenced

- **ADR-008 [#8 Reliability Scoring]** `CONFIRMED` The three-window rolling score in a single authoritative microservice is definitively the correct V2 design. PeerTrust confirms that any distributed score aggregation mechanism requires pairwise interaction data between evaluating nodes and the peers they rate — data that does not exist in Vyomanaut's architecture. The paper's most important validation is negative: it shows precisely what inputs are required for a distributed reputation system to work, and those inputs are absent in V2. The centralised model is not a compromise; it is the only model compatible with the audit architecture. *Because:* The PSM and TVM metrics both require `S(u,i)` — satisfaction ratings from direct transactions between peer w and the peers who have rated peer u. Section 4.1 explains that this requires each peer to maintain feedback data about its own transaction partners. Vyomanaut providers have no such data about each other.
- **ADR-024 [#18 Economic Mechanism]** `INFORMED — NEW PARAMETERS FOR PENALTY STRUCTURE` PeerTrust's adaptive time-window algorithm provides a concrete design template for Vyomanaut's penalty graduation question (Q29-1). The dual-window approach (full window win=100 transactions vs. adaptive window wins=20) maps to a graduated escrow penalty structure: compute a reliability score over the full 30-day window and over a recent 7-day window; if the 7-day score drops below the 30-day score by a threshold, treat the provider as actively deteriorating and apply a partial hold on earnings immediately rather than waiting for the 72h departure threshold. This is graduated penalty without requiring new ADR machinery — it uses the existing three-window score (ADR-008) as input to the payment release decision. ADR-024 should incorporate this as a candidate parameter for the penalty graduation design (Q29-1). The paper also confirms that making reputation hard to build and easy to lose — through the adaptive window — is the correct deterrence structure for strategic providers. This maps to Vyomanaut's held-earnings model: earnings built up over months are at risk of partial seizure even for short deterioration events, not only at full 72h departure. *Because:* Section 4.3 shows that the adaptive algorithm makes the cost of rebuilding reputation higher than the gain from milking it, which is the same property Vyomanaut's escrow model must achieve: making strategic departure unprofitable.
- **ADR-014 [#19 Adversarial Defences]** `CONFIRMED` The paper's collusion simulations (Figure 2b, Figure 4b) show that without PSM-style detection, a collusive group can completely fool a reputation system even at small adversarial percentages. This validates why Vyomanaut's structural collusion defense — the 20% ASN cap at placement time — must remain the primary control rather than attempting to detect collusion in the scoring layer after the fact. PeerTrust PSM detects collusion retrospectively through feedback divergence; Vyomanaut eliminates the collusion impact prospectively through placement constraints. The paper confirms that retrospective detection alone is insufficient when the colluding group can manufacture enough fake interaction history. *Because:* Figure 4b shows transaction success rate drops to 0 for both conventional and TVM approaches under collusion — the system is made worse than having no reputation mechanism at all. The structural prevention approach in ADR-014 is confirmed as the correct first-line defense.

## Disagreements

- **EigenTrust (Kamvar et al., Paper 24):** PeerTrust's Section 7 notes that EigenTrust requires pre-trusted peers and that its implementation demands strong coordination and synchronisation between peers. PeerTrust PSM avoids the pre-trusted peer requirement by using personalised similarity, which is a stronger robustness guarantee under arbitrary adversarial conditions. *Implication for us:* Both papers independently confirm that the microservice registration gate plays the role that either pre-trusted peers (EigenTrust) or PKI identity (PeerTrust) must play in a P2P reputation system. Our registration gating closes the bootstrapping problem both papers identify.
- **eBay's binary reputation system (discussed in Section 3.1):** Simple sum of +1/0/-1 ratings is identified as fundamentally flawed because it rewards volume over quality. Dellarocas (2001) is cited: binary mechanisms produce unfair market outcomes. *Implication for us:* Vyomanaut's three-window rolling audit pass rate is a ratio (passes/total), not a sum. It inherits the normalisation the paper argues is mandatory. The 24h/7d/30d windows provide the temporal sensitivity that PeerTrust's adaptive window provides.
