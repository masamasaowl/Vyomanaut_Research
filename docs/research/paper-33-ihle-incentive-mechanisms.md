## Paper 33 — Incentive Mechanisms in Peer-to-Peer Networks — A Systematic Literature Review

**Authors:** Cornelius Ihle, Dennis Trautwein, Moritz Schubotz, Norman Meuschke, Bela Gipp — University of Göttingen / FIZ Karlsruhe **Venue / Year:** ACM Computing Surveys, Vol. 56, No. 1 | January 2023 **Topics:** #18, #8, #13, #19 **ADRs produced:** none — ADR-024 gains concrete design constraints and is now ready to be written; ADR-008, ADR-011, ADR-012 confirmed; see Decisions Influenced

---

### Problem Solved

Decentralised P2P networks require incentive mechanisms to coordinate participation and prevent free-riding, but the field's literature is fragmented across monetary, reputation, and service reward types, covering over a dozen distinct network types (MANETs, DTNs, MSNs, DLT, general P2P) with no common evaluation framework. Prior reviews covered only ad-hoc mobile networks or narrowly defined sub-problems, and none systematically covered DLT-based incentives. This survey reviews 105 primary papers and 11 secondary reviews published between 1993 and 2022, synthesises a five-dimension framework (reward type, attack resistance, data management, contribution model, network type), and produces a decision tree for selecting the correct incentive type for a given use case. For Vyomanaut, the paper provides the final prerequisite for ADR-024: it maps the full design space for monetary P2P incentives, identifies which auction and deterministic pricing patterns have been deployed in storage contexts, and confirms through the decision tree that fiat monetary incentives with an accumulated history (escrow) are the structurally correct choice for a system where resources must match demand over time and service contingents need not be transferable.

---

### Key Findings

**Three-category incentive taxonomy and their fundamental trade-offs (Section 3.1):** Monetary rewards are transferable between identities and can accumulate — the core property that enables escrow. Their transferability creates a double-spending risk that requires either a central broker or DLT to prevent. Reputation rewards are identity-bound and cannot be transferred; a peer cannot sell its reputation to another. Service rewards grant immediate access without tracking history — they are the simplest to implement and most resistant to Sybil attacks (an identity with no history is denied service immediately), but cannot motivate participation outside real-time interactions.

**Decision tree for incentive type selection (Figure 4, Section 5):** The tree has four binary decisions in order: (1) Does the service need to be decentralised? → yes. (2) Can you ensure nodes will participate without a monetary mechanism? → no (home desktop providers without financial stakes will not run storage daemons reliably). (3) Do you want to allow accumulated service contingents (earnings built up over time)? → yes (audit-pass-based payment accumulates earnings). (4) Do you want service contingents to be transferable? → no (providers should not be able to sell their escrow balance to other accounts — this would enable attacks). The decision tree unambiguously selects reputation as the answer when contingents are non-transferable. The paper defines reputation broadly as any identity-bound, non-transferable accumulated history metric. Vyomanaut's held-earnings escrow is monetary in payment mechanism but reputation-like in its non-transferability constraint — it is a hybrid that the survey calls a "deterministic monetary incentive supplemented by reputation."

**Deterministic vs. auction monetary pricing (Section 3.1.1, 4.1.1):** Deterministic pricing guarantees predictable costs for providers and data owners — a critical property for a contracted storage service where both parties need to know in advance what they will pay and earn. Auction mechanisms achieve fairer pricing dynamically but introduce variable costs that are unacceptable when a provider has committed disk space under a long-term contract. Gramaglia et al.'s offline incentive mechanism for long-term P2P backup storage (Table 3) is the closest surveyed system to Vyomanaut: it uses a deterministic micro-payment scheme with delayed settlements through digital cheques, and explicitly acknowledges that providers can be offline for extended periods.

**White-washing and entry fee as collusion defences (Sections 3.2, A.4):** White-washing — rejoining under a new identity to reset a bad reputation — is universally identified as an attack that open (permissionless) networks cannot prevent without access control or an entry barrier. The survey identifies two mitigations: cryptographic identity requirements that make key generation costly, and registration-based onboarding (KYC). Goswami et al.'s mechanism uses an entry fee to prevent white-washing, exploiting that financially-incentivised networks can charge new entrants a provable stake before admitting them. Vyomanaut's registration gating (ADR-001, ADR-005) is exactly this mechanism.

**Held-earnings escrow as the correct architecture for storage incentives (Section 4.1.3, Gramaglia):** The survey's single paper on long-term P2P backup storage (Gramaglia et al., 2012) uses delayed payments held by a central broker until the provider has delivered the contracted storage. The paper explicitly notes that bank-managed currency requires registration and may exclude providers from certain jurisdictions — the exact problem Vyomanaut solves by using Razorpay/UPI instead of cryptocurrency. This validates that the Vyomanaut escrow model (ADR-011) is the architecturally correct monetary mechanism for contracted cold storage at the target provider demographic.

**Attack resistance landscape (Figure 2):** Selfish attacks (free-riding) are addressed by 66 of the surveyed mechanisms — they are the dominant concern. Collusion attacks are mitigated in 13 mechanisms and the field has not converged on a solution. Only 6 mechanisms are fully resistant to collusion. Identity attacks are mitigated in 11 mechanisms. Sniffing and confidentiality attacks receive the least attention — most mechanisms do not address them at all. This confirms that Vyomanaut's layered defence strategy (selfish attack: audit-pass payment; collusion: 20% ASN cap; identity: registration gating; confidentiality: AONT-RS client-side encryption) covers the full attack surface that the field typically leaves partially unaddressed.

**Openness vs. access control (Section 4.2.3, Figure 3):** 70 of 77 surveyed mechanisms are fully open — they do not require registration or preconditions for participation. The 7 that use access control are all either consortium blockchain systems or bank-managed schemes. The survey notes that openness benefits decentralisation and maximises contribution rate, but open designs are inherently more vulnerable to Sybil and white-washing attacks. Vyomanaut's registration-gated admission is in the minority — the survey's data confirms this is an acceptable trade-off when the goal is deterring adversarial behaviour rather than maximising participation.

### Trade-offs

| Chosen | Over | Consequence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Deterministic monetary pricing (fixed per-audit payout) | Auction pricing (dynamic per-unit bids) | Predictable earnings for providers and costs for data owners; no price discovery mechanism; may over- or under-pay relative to market rate |
| Held-earnings escrow (non-transferable, accumulated) | Pre-committed collateral (stake before earning) | No capital barrier for entry — suitable for Indian home desktop providers; deterrent to exit rather than deterrent to entry; providers with few chunks have little at risk early |
| Registration gating (KYC/phone number) | Open participation | Prevents Sybil and white-washing attacks; reduces network growth rate; excludes participants without a registered phone number |
| Monetary reward (fiat UPI) | Reputation-only reward (service access) | Transferable earnings motivate participation without requiring ongoing network dependency; creates double-spending risk managed by microservice |
| Single-task incentive (audit pass = earning) | Multi-task incentive (storage + bandwidth + latency rewarded separately) | Simple accounting; providers optimise for audit pass rate rather than holistic service quality; multi-task incentive is deferred to V3 hot band |

### Breaks in Our Case

- **Survey's decision tree recommends reputation (non-transferable) for use cases with non-transferable accumulated contingents** ≠ **Vyomanaut uses fiat monetary escrow as the payment mechanism while making escrow non-transferable by policy** → The decision tree result is correct at the design-intent level: Vyomanaut's escrow is non-transferable and identity-bound (ADR-011), making it functionally equivalent to a reputation metric that happens to be denominated in rupees. The escrow is paid out, not transferred — a provider cannot sell their earned balance to another account. This hybrid property is not covered as a named pattern in the survey, but it is consistent with the survey's finding that pure monetary incentives often require supplementary reputation mechanisms to prevent strategic gaming.
- **Survey's closest P2P storage paper (Gramaglia et al., 2012) uses banks as the currency manager** ≠ **Vyomanaut uses Razorpay/Cashfree (UPI payment gateway) with no bank-to-bank registration requirement**→ Gramaglia explicitly notes that bank-managed currency may exclude providers from other jurisdictions. Razorpay UPI is available to any Indian bank account holder, which eliminates this exclusion for V2's India-first target population. The architecture maps directly: Gramaglia's bank ≈ Vyomanaut's microservice + Razorpay payment provider.
- **Survey models primarily unincentivised P2P networks (file-sharing, streaming) or blockchain networks with token economies** ≠ **Vyomanaut is a fiat-escrow contracted storage network with pre-agreed SLAs and a vetting period** → None of the 105 surveyed papers model a system with all three of: (a) fiat currency payment, (b) a vetting period before full earning begins, and (c) a held-earnings seizure mechanism for SLA violations. The closest analogues are Filecoin (pre-committed collateral, not fiat) and Gramaglia (delayed fiat payments, no vetting). Vyomanaut's economic mechanism design has no direct empirical precedent in the survey — it must be designed from first principles using the framework this paper provides.
- **Survey finds that 75 of 77 incentive mechanisms with contribution data are either fully reactive or both reactive and proactive** ≠ **Vyomanaut's audit schedule is microservice-driven, not provider-initiated** → In most surveyed systems, providers proactively contribute resources and receive rewards based on observed contribution. In Vyomanaut, the microservice initiates audit challenges — providers cannot choose when to be evaluated. This is closer to a reactive model from the provider's perspective (they respond to challenges) and a proactive model from the microservice's perspective. The contribution model maps cleanly to the existing classification but with an inverted direction of initiation.
- **Survey notes that multitasking incentives (rewarding multiple task types) are present in only 30 of 85 surveyed mechanisms** ≠ **Vyomanaut V2 rewards only one task (audit pass); storage capacity, latency, and bandwidth are not separately incentivised** → Single-task incentive is the majority pattern and acceptable for V2. The survey confirms that multi-task incentives add complexity without necessarily improving performance unless the tasks are genuinely heterogeneous. V2's cold storage is a homogeneous single-task system; multi-task incentives are a V3 consideration for the hot band (ADR-018).

### Decisions Influenced

- **ADR-024 [#18 Economic Mechanism]** `PARTIALLY CLOSED — DESIGN SPACE NOW FULLY BOUNDED` The survey provides the final framework inputs needed to write ADR-024. Four concrete constraints follow from this paper, combined with Filecoin (Paper 29) and PeerTrust (Paper 31): (1) **Deterministic pricing is the correct choice.**Auction mechanisms are appropriate for commodity markets with flexible pricing; contracted cold storage with month-long SLAs requires predictable pricing. The price is set at contract creation and does not fluctuate with network demand. (2) **Escrow non-transferability must be enforced at the payment service level.** The decision tree confirms that a held-earnings model with non-transferable balance is a hybrid monetary-reputation scheme. The microservice must refuse escrow transfer requests — a provider cannot assign their escrow balance to another provider_id. (3) **Vetting period maps to the "open with entry cost" design pattern.** The survey's evidence that open systems without barriers suffer Sybil and white-washing attacks validates Vyomanaut's 4–6 month vetting period as the correct design. The vetting period is the "entry cost" — not financial, but temporal. (4) **Penalty structure: graduated over time, binary at the 72h threshold.** The survey's data on selfish attack resistance (66 mechanisms use some form of graduated reputation decay before binary exclusion) confirms that the three-window score (ADR-008) feeding into a graduated hold at 48h and seizure at 72h (Q29-1 from Filecoin) is the dominant industry pattern. ADR-024 should adopt: score decrement per 24h polling cycle (existing), partial earnings hold trigger at the first 48h absence, full seizure at 72h. ADR-024 remains Proposed (requires a product decision on pricing tier and held-earnings percentage) but the design space is now fully bounded by Papers 29, 31, and 32 together. *Because:* Section 5's decision tree unambiguously selects the monetary+non-transferable hybrid for Vyomanaut's use case; Section 4.1.1's survey of deterministic P2P storage mechanisms validates delayed-payment escrow as the production-proven pattern.
- **ADR-011 [#13 Fiat Escrow]** `CONFIRMED` The survey confirms that the only deployed non-blockchain monetary incentives in P2P storage use either banks (Gramaglia 2012) or centrally managed currencies. Razorpay/UPI is architecturally equivalent to Gramaglia's bank-managed cheque scheme but without geographic restrictions. The fiat-over-crypto decision is validated: no surveyed paper shows a cryptocurrency-based storage incentive outperforming fiat equivalents on provider participation rate. *Because:* Table 3 shows that centrally managed currency (CMC) is the pattern used in all deterministic storage incentive mechanisms. Blockchain-enabled currency (BEC) appears only in auction mechanisms where trustless settlement is the primary goal — not applicable to contracted SLA-based storage.
- **ADR-012 [#13 Payment per Audit Passed]** `CONFIRMED` The survey's finding that selfish attacks (free-riding) receive the most attention in incentive mechanism design, and that audit-pass payment is the most direct mechanism to prevent free-riding in storage systems (Section 4.1.1, Gramaglia; Section 4.1.3, Shen et al.), confirms the per-audit payment model. Shen et al.'s equity-based incentive for persistent virtual world content storage uses replica groups with reliability tiers — the closest surveyed system to Vyomanaut's audit-pass model — and shows effective free-rider elimination. *Because:* Figure 2 shows 46 mechanisms are resistant to selfish attacks and 20 mitigate them; audit-pass-contingent payment is structurally in the "resistant" category because a provider cannot free-ride (earn without storing) without being able to compute a valid PoR response.
- **ADR-008 [#8 Reliability Scoring]** `CONFIRMED` The survey's data on reputation mechanisms confirms that individual reciprocity (tracking history per provider) is the dominant pattern (Table 4: 26 of 48 reputation mechanisms use individual reciprocity). The three-window rolling score (ADR-008) is a standard individual reciprocity mechanism. The survey also confirms that global denylisting — removing a provider from the network permanently — is rare (only 5 of 48 mechanisms) and requires a single source of truth, which the microservice provides. This validates the design where score decrement leads to lower assignment priority, not immediate ejection. *Because:* Section 4.1.2 shows that gradual service degradation (reducing assignment priority for lower-scored providers) outperforms binary denylist in networks where participation must be maintained during recovery periods.

### Disagreements

- **Blockchain-centric view of monetary incentives in the survey's primary literature:** 14 of 31 auction mechanisms in Table 3 use blockchain-enabled currency. The survey treats DLT as a promising single source of truth for reputation management, noting that Kang et al.'s blockchain-based reputation outperforms traditional approaches. Vyomanaut rejects blockchain entirely on the grounds of on-chain write latency, gas cost, and Indian regulatory uncertainty. *Implication for us:* The microservice provides the single source of truth that DLT would otherwise supply. The survey's data confirms the functional role — it does not prescribe DLT as the only implementation.
- **Gramaglia et al. (2012):** Uses a bank-managed delayed payment system and explicitly requires registration with a bank, potentially excluding providers from certain jurisdictions. The paper notes this as a limitation. *Implication for us:* Razorpay/UPI solves the jurisdiction problem for India. The architectural pattern transfers; the implementation swaps banks for a payment gateway accessible to any Indian bank account holder.
